Introduction

This case presents a clear pattern of malicious prosecution and abuse of process, stemming from the plaintiff's deliberate manipulation of legal proceedings.

The defendant's constitutional rights have been systematically violated through a calculated campaign of misrepresentation that perverts the justice system for personal gain. The proceedings have become so detached from their protective purpose that they are best described as "Kafkaesque"—a term courts use to describe proceedings "totally lacking in notice and a meaningful opportunity to be heard."

I cannot agree as would justify an arrest or conviction. On the contrary, I think that the policeman's action was a "deliberate defiance" of ordinary official duty as well as of the constitutional right of free speech. For at least where time allows, courtesy and explanation of commands are basic elements of good official conduct in a democratic society. Here petitioner was "asked" then "told" then "commanded" to stop speaking, but a man making a lawful address is certainly not required to be silent merely [340 U.S. 315, 328] because an officer directs it. Petitioner was entitled to know why he should cease doing a lawful act. Not once was he told. I understand that people in authoritarian countries must obey arbitrary orders. I had hoped that there was no such duty in the United States. In my judgment, today's holding means that as a practical matter, minority speakers can be silenced in any city. Hereafter, despite the First and Fourteenth Amendments, the policeman's club can take heavy toll of a current administration's public critics. Criticism of public officials will be too dangerous for all but the most courageous. This is true regardless of the fact that in [340 U.S. 315, 329] two other cases decided this day, Kunz v. New York, 340 U.S. 290 ; Niemotko v. Maryland, 340 U.S. 268 , a majority, in obedience to past decisions of this Court, provides a theoretical safeguard for freedom of speech. For whatever is thought to be guaranteed in Kunz and Niemotko is taken away by what is done here. The three cases read together mean that while previous restraints probably cannot be imposed on an unpopular speaker, the police have discretion to silence him as soon as the customary hostility to his views develops.

But the people of this nation have ordained in the light of history, that, in spite of the probability of excesses and abuses, these liberties are, in the long view, essential to enlightened opinion and right conduct on the part of the citizens of a democracy." Cantwell v. Connecticut, 310 U.S. 296, 310

Misconduct & Concealment of Evidence

A prosecutor's constitutional duty is to disclose all material exculpatory evidence to the defense. This is not merely a procedural formality but a cornerstone of a fair trial, intended to prevent one-sided justice.

Core Principle: The Duty to Disclose

The prosecution's role is not simply to win but to see "that justice shall be done." This is frustrated by tactics aimed at securing a favorable outcome at any cost by concealing evidence that could exonerate the accused or impeach the accuser's credibility.

Brady v. Maryland, 373 U.S. 83 (1963)

Malicious Prosecution

A claim for malicious prosecution arises when legal action is initiated for an improper purpose and without a reasonable basis. The plaintiff's selective presentation of evidence—cherry-picking communications while concealing the defendant's documented abuse reports—vitiates any claim of probable cause.

"Half of the truth may amount to a lie if it is understood to be the whole." — Prosser & Keeton, The Law of Torts

Case Study: Feiner v. New York

A central issue in this matter is whether speech can be curtailed because of a hostile reaction from listeners. The landmark case Feiner v. New York established the "heckler's veto" doctrine.

Majority Opinion

The Court held that Feiner was not arrested for the content of his speech, but for the dangerous reaction it engendered. When a speaker "passes the bounds of argument or persuasion and undertakes incitement to riot," police have a duty to prevent breach of peace.

Procedural Violations

The plaintiff's conduct demonstrates a systematic pattern of procedural abuse designed to deny the defendant fundamental rights.

Notice Rights

Plaintiff initiated hearings without proper notice, denying the defendant opportunity to prepare.

Vague Allegations

Petition relied on general comments without dates, times, or places—insufficient for cause of action.

Meaningful Hearing

Court's failure to inquire into disputed facts deprived defendant of constitutional protections.

The right to procedural due process is considered "absolute" in that it does not depend upon the merits of a claimant's substantive assertions, and its observance is critical to organized society. It has been established that the denial of procedural due process should be actionable for nominal damages without proof of actual injury. Specifically, the Court has held that if, upon remand, it is determined that respondents' suspensions were justified, they are nevertheless entitled to recover nominal damages not to exceed one dollar from petitioners [Id. at 266-267 (citation omitted)]. Thus, even if the plaintiffs would have been suspended had they received due process, they may recover nominal damages based on the denial of due process. However, this does not imply that a person can incur "harm" under the Due Process Clause absent a deprivation of property. Damages under the Due Process Clause are contingent upon a deprivation of property, as noted in Board of Regents of State Colleges v. Roth, 408 U.S. 564, 576 (1972), where the Fourteenth Amendment's procedural protection of property is described as a safeguard of the security of interests already acquired in specific benefits.

Conclusion

The plaintiff's systematic concealment of evidence, misrepresentation of facts, and misuse of protective-order proceedings constitute a profound abuse of the legal system.

These actions violate the defendant's constitutional rights and subvert the protective purpose of the law. Justice requires a remedial order to shield the defendant from this malicious campaign and restore integrity to the process.
